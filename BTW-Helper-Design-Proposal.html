01 — Positioning & anti-framing

Sell relief, not bookkeeping.

The category everyone else competes in is "boekhoudsoftware." That word does the freelancer's anxiety a favour — it tells them they're about to become an accountant. We refuse the category.

BTW Helper is positioned against admin itself. The enemy isn't MoneyBird; it's the Sunday-night dread of the deadline, the fear of the wrong box, the €600 you pay someone to make it go away. We don't promise features — we promise the feeling on the other side of "filed."

◆ Head of Strategy · why anti-framing

Freelancers don't aspire to do bookkeeping; they tolerate it. Positioning as "accounting software, but easier" still anchors us to a chore. Positioning as "your BTW, handled" anchors us to an outcome. Outcomes convert; chores get postponed.

Concretely: every headline leads with the result ("filed," "done," "clear"), never the mechanism ("classify," "calculate," "manage"). The mechanism is proof, shown lower on the page.

02 — Naming

A name that sounds like the end of a task.

The name should be Dutch enough to signal "this is for the Dutch system," short enough to own, and emotionally pointed at the moment of done. Three candidates plus the working name, with a clear recommendation.

Klaar
Dutch for "done / ready." The exact word a freelancer wants to say about their BTW. "Je BTW? Klaar." One syllable, emotional, ownable, and it works as an exclamation in all three markets. Risk: common word, so the brand must do the trademark/SEO lifting — solved with a wordmark and "Klaar — BTW" lockup.
Recommended
Kwartaal
Dutch for "quarter." Names the filing rhythm precisely; instantly legible to the market. Slightly more functional and less emotional than Klaar — describes when, not the relief. Strong, safe second choice.
Strong alt

Dutch for "clear." Speaks straight to the core pain (confusion → clarity) and sounds premium. Risk: used broadly in finance/insurance branding, so differentiation is harder.
Alt
BTW Helper
The working name. Maximally descriptive and SEO-friendly — does exactly what it says. Keep as the explanatory tagline even if the brand name changes: "Klaar — your BTW helper."
Keep as descriptor
◆ Head of Strategy · recommendation

Lead with Klaar, retain "BTW Helper" as the descriptive sub-line for clarity and search. It's the only candidate that is itself the outcome we're selling. If trademark conflicts block it, fall back to Kwartaal. The landing copy below is written so either name drops in cleanly.

◆ Head of Design · why this type system

Space Grotesk for display: geometric, European, slightly idiosyncratic — it reads as "made by someone with taste," which is the exact audience (creative freelancers). Every competitor defaults to Inter or DM Sans everywhere; using a characterful display face is a cheap, instant way to not look like them.

JetBrains Mono for every euro figure and rubriek code is the deliberate risk: it makes the numbers feel like a precision instrument, like a fiscal terminal. Money in monospace signals "exact, trustworthy, machine-checked" — which is the entire promise. It also visually separates "the scary tax numbers" from "the friendly human copy," so the page guides the eye between reassurance and proof.

Inter stays for body because personality should live in two places, not everywhere. Boldness spent in one spot lands harder than boldness sprayed across the page.

◆ Head of Design · why the live dashboard is the hero

The most persuasive thing we can show is the product doing the scary part. A freelancer's stomach drops at "how much do I owe?" — so the hero answers it, on screen, with a number animating into place (GSAP count-up) over a subtle 3D ambient field (Three.js). It's the thesis of the whole product in one glance: this is what relief looks like.

This is why we don't open with a headline-over-gradient template. The characteristic artifact of this product's world is the result of a filing — so that's the hero. Everything else on the page is evidence for that one promise.

06 — FAQ (objection handling)

The five things they need answered before "yes."

Is this an accountant?
No — and that's the point. BTW Helper is a filing assistant, not an accountant or bookkeeping suite. It prepares your aangifte so you can file it yourself in minutes. For complex situations you'd still see a professional; for routine quarterly BTW, you won't need one.
How do I know the classification is correct?
The AI explains every invoice in plain language — why it's a 4a reverse charge, why a US invoice gets self-assessed at 21%. Built-in consistency checks flag anything that doesn't add up, and you review every number before it goes anywhere. Nothing is hidden.
Does it submit to the Belastingdienst automatically?
No. By design, it pre-fills and gives you copy buttons — you paste into the official portal and submit yourself. Automatic submission to a tax authority is a risk we won't take on your behalf. You stay in control of the final click.
Is my financial data safe?
Your data is isolated to your account, encrypted in transit and at rest, and stored on EU infrastructure. One account belongs to one person and one business — no shared logins, no other user can ever see your invoices.
What if my invoices are messy — foreign, mixed VAT, refunds?
That's exactly what it's built for. EU and non-EU vendors, currency conversion, reverse charge, foreign VAT and credit notes are all handled. Edge cases get flagged for your review rather than silently guessed.
